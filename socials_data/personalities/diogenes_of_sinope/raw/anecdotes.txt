When Alexander the Great addressed him with greetings and asked if he wanted anything, Diogenes replied, "Yes, stand a little out of my sun."

When someone asked him how he could avoid being angry with other people, he replied, "By becoming good yourself."

Seeing a child drinking out of his hands, he threw away his cup from his wallet saying, "A child has beaten me in plainness of living."

He used to say that other men lived to eat, but he ate to live.

When asked where he came from, he said, "I am a citizen of the world (kosmopolites)."

He lit a lamp in broad daylight and said, as he went about, "I am looking for a man."

When someone struck him with a beam and then said, "Look out," Diogenes struck him with his staff and said, "Look out."

He once begged alms of a statue, and when asked why he did so, he replied, "To get practice in being refused."

When asked what was the right time to marry, he replied, "For a young man not yet: for an old man never at all."

When asked what wine he found the most pleasant to drink, he replied, "That for which other people pay."

Plato defined man as an animal, biped and featherless, and was applauded. Diogenes plucked a fowl and brought it into the lecture-room with the words, "Here is Plato's man."

When someone said to him, "Most people laugh at you," he replied, "And perhaps the asses laugh at them; but just as they do not care for the asses, so neither do I care for them."

He saw a woman bowing before the gods in an ungraceful attitude, and wishing to free her of superstition, he went up to her and said, "Are you not afraid, my good woman, that a god may be standing behind you (for all things are full of his presence) and you may be put to shame?"

When asked what he had gained from philosophy, he replied, "If nothing else, at least to be prepared for every fortune."

Seeing a youth dressing with elaborate care, he said, "If it's for men, it's unfortunate; if for women, it's unjust."
