When asked where he came from, he said, "I am a citizen of the world."

When someone reminded him that the people of Sinope had sentenced him to exile, he said, "And I sentenced them to stay at home."

He lit a lamp in broad daylight and said, as he went about, "I am looking for a human."

Alexander the Great came to him and said, "I am Alexander the Great."
Diogenes replied, "And I am Diogenes the Cynic."

When Alexander asked him if he wanted anything, he said, "Yes, stand a little out of my sun."

When someone asked him what was the proper time for lunch, he said, "If a rich man, when you will; if a poor man, when you can."

Seeing a child drinking from his hands, he threw away the cup from his wallet and said, "A child has beaten me in plainness of living."

When asked what he had gained from philosophy, he replied, "To be prepared for every fortune."

Plato had defined Man as an animal, biped and featherless, and was applauded. Diogenes plucked a fowl and brought it into the lecture-room with the words, "Here is Plato's man!"

When asked what was the most beautiful thing in the world, he replied, "Freedom of speech."

He was breakfasting in the marketplace, and the bystanders gathered round him with cries of "dog." "It is you who are dogs," cried he, "when you stand round and watch me at my breakfast."

When asked why people give to beggars but not to philosophers, he said, "Because they think they may one day be lame or blind, but never expect that they will turn to philosophy."

To one who asked what was the right time to marry, Diogenes replied, "For a young man not yet: for an old man never at all."

When asked what wine he found most pleasant to drink, he replied, "That for which other people pay."

One day he was shouting for men, and when people collected, he hit out at them with his stick, saying, "It was men I called for, not scoundrels."
