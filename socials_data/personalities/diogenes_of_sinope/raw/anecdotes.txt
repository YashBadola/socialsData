Diogenes was knee-deep in a river, washing greens. Plato passed by and said, "If you had paid your respects to Dionysius, you would not be washing greens." Diogenes replied, "If you washed greens, you would not be paying respects to Dionysius."

When Alexander the Great visited Diogenes, he asked, "Is there any favor I can do for you?" Diogenes replied, "Yes, stand out of my sunlight."

Someone took him into a magnificent house and warned him not to spit, whereupon having cleared his throat he spat into the man's face, being unable, he said, to find a meaner receptacle.

When asked what sort of wine he liked best, he replied, "That which belongs to another."

He lit a lamp in broad daylight and said, as he went about, "I am looking for an honest man."

When Plato gave the definition of man as "featherless biped," Diogenes plucked a chicken and brought it into Plato's Academy, saying, "Behold! I've brought you a man."

Seeing a child drinking from his hands, he threw away his cup and said, "A child has beaten me in plainness of living."

When asked why he went about begging of statues, he replied, "To get practice in being refused."

To one who asked what was the proper time for lunch, he said, "If a rich man, when you will; if a poor man, when you can."

He was once captured by pirates and sold as a slave. When the auctioneer asked him what he was proficient in, he replied, "In ruling men." Thereupon he pointed to a certain Corinthian, named Xeniades, and said, "Sell me to this man; he needs a master."

When asked where he came from, he said, "I am a citizen of the world (kosmopolitÄ“s)."

He used to say that other dogs bite their enemies, but I bite my friends to save them.

Seeing an archer who was a bad shot, he sat down beside the target. When asked why, he said, "In order not to get hit."
