When Alexander the Great came to visit Diogenes in Corinth, he found him sunning himself. Alexander stood over him and said, "Ask of me whatever you wish." Diogenes replied, "Stand a little out of my sun."

Plato defined man as a featherless biped and was applauded. Diogenes plucked a chicken and brought it into the lecture room, crying, "Behold! Plato's man!" So Plato added to his definition, "having broad nails."

Diogenes lit a lamp in broad daylight and went about saying, "I am looking for a human."

When asked why he begged from statues, Diogenes replied, "To get practice in being rejected."

Diogenes saw a child drinking from his hands, and threw away his cup, saying, "A child has beaten me in plainness of living."

When someone reproached him with his exile, saying, "The people of Sinope sentenced you to exile," Diogenes replied, "And I sentenced them to stay home."

Asked where he came from, he said, "I am a citizen of the world."

Seeing a young man acting the dandy, he said, "Are you not ashamed, that your own nature should differ for the worse from that of a woman?"

When asked what was the right time to marry, he replied, "For a young man not yet: for an old man never at all."

Of a rich man who was unlearned he said, "He is a sheep with a golden fleece."

To one who asked what was the proper time for lunch, he said, "If a rich man, when you will; if a poor man, when you can."

When asked what he had gained from philosophy, he replied, "This at least, if nothing else -- to be prepared for every fortune."
