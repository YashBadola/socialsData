The Sayings and Anecdotes of Diogenes of Sinope

When someone asked him what was the proper time for lunch, he said, "If a rich man, when you will; if a poor man, when you can."

He was observing a child drinking out of his hands, and threw away the cup from his wallet, saying, "A child has beaten me in plainness of living."

When Alexander the Great stood over him and asked, "Ask of me whatever you want," he replied, "Stand out of my sunlight."

Someone took him into a magnificent house and warned him not to spit, whereupon, having cleared his throat, he spat into the man's face, saying that he could not find a meaner receptacle.

He lit a lamp in broad daylight and said, as he went about, "I am looking for a human."

When asked what was the most beautiful thing in the world, he replied, "Freedom of speech."

Plato defined man as a featherless biped and was applauded. Diogenes plucked a chicken and brought it into the lecture room, saying, "Here is Plato's man!" Consequently, the definition was added to, "With broad flat nails."

When asked from where he came, he said, "I am a citizen of the world (Cosmopolite)."

Seeing a youth dressing with elaborate care, he said, "If it's for men, you're mistaken; if for women, it's unjust."

To one who asked what was the right time to marry, Diogenes replied, "For a young man not yet: for an old man never at all."

When asked what kind of wine he found pleasant to drink, he answered, "That for which other people pay."

He said that he was begging for a livelihood, not for a gift.

When he was exposed for sale as a slave, he pointed to a certain Corinthian, very well dressed, and said, "Sell me to this man; he needs a master."

He used to roll his tub up and down the Cranion in the heat of summer; and in the winter he used to embrace statues covered with snow, using every means of inuring himself to hardship.

He once begged alms of a statue, and, when asked why he did so, replied, "To get practice in being refused."

When asked why people give to beggars but not to philosophers, he said, "Because they think they may one day be lame or blind, but never expect that they will turn to philosophy."

To a man who said, "I am not calculated for philosophy," he answered, "Why then do you live, if you have no desire to live properly?"

He saw a woman bowing before the gods in an unseemly attitude, and wishing to rid her of her superstition, as Zoilus relates, he went up to her and said, "Are you not afraid, my good woman, that a god may be standing behind you (for all things are full of his presence) and you may be put to shame?"

When asked what benefit he reaped from philosophy, he replied, "If nothing else, at least to be prepared for every fortune."

Being asked what was the most wretched thing in life, he answered, "An old man destitute."

When asked what creature bites the worst, he said, "Of wild beasts, the sycophant; of tame ones, the flatterer."

Seeing a child throwing stones at a cross (or gallows), he said, "Well done, you will hit your mark."

To a young man who complained that he was ill-suited for philosophy, he said, "Why then do you live, if you do not care to live well?"

He once saw the officials of a temple leading away some one who had stolen a bowl belonging to the treasurers, and said, "The great thieves are leading away the little thief."

One day he was eating olives and dried figs, when Plato came by and said "Diogenes, if you had learned to be subservient to the king (Dionysius), you would not have to live on olives." Diogenes replied, "And you, Plato, if you had learned to live on olives, would not have to be subservient to the king."

He used to say that other dogs bit their enemies, but he bit his friends to save them.

When asked how he would like to be buried, he said, "Face downwards." When asked why, he said, "Because in a little while everything will be turned upside down."

Seeing a fop dressing with great care, he said, "Is it for a man? Then it is wasted. Is it for a woman? Then it is unjust."

He was asked what was the most beautiful thing among men. He said, "Freedom of speech."

Of a remarkably dirty bath he asked, "When people wash here, where do they go to get clean?"

He saw some women hanging on olive trees (after hanging themselves). He said, "Would that all trees bore such fruit."

When some one reminded him that the people of Sinope had sentenced him to exile, he said, "And I sentenced them to stay at home."

He was asked what was the heaviest burden on the earth. He replied, "An uneducated man."

When asked what he gained from philosophy, he said, "To be rich without a penny."

Seeing a young man behaving effeminately, he said, "Are you not ashamed that nature made you a man and you are forcing yourself to play the woman?"

He cried out, "Hey, men!" and when people collected, he hit them with his stick, saying, "I called for men, not scoundrels."

To one who asked, "What is the result of your philosophy?" he replied, "To be prepared for any fortune."

When asked what wine he liked best, he replied, "Another's."

He saw an unskilled archer practicing, and went and sat down by the target. When asked why, he said, "To avoid being hit."

He once went into a theatre while everyone else was leaving, and when asked why, he said, "It is what I have been practicing all my life."

"I am Diogenes the Dog. I nuzzle the kind, bark at the greedy, and bite scoundrels."

"Discourse on virtue and they pass by like blind men. Talk of money and they prick up their ears like donkeys."

"The foundation of every state is the education of its youth."

"We have two ears and one tongue so that we would listen more and talk less."

"It is not that I am mad, it is only that my head is different from yours."

"Blushing is the color of virtue."

"Dogs and philosophers do the greatest good and get the fewest rewards."

"Man is the most intelligent of the animals - and the most silly."
