When Alexander the Great visited Diogenes, he found him resting in the sunlight. Alexander, standing over him, said, "Ask of me whatever you wish." Diogenes replied, "Stand a little out of my sun." Alexander was so struck by this, and admired so much the haughtiness and grandeur of the man who had nothing but scorn for him, that he said to his followers, who were laughing and mocking the philosopher as they went away, "But truly, if I were not Alexander, I would be Diogenes."

Diogenes lit a lamp in broad daylight and went about saying, "I am looking for a man."

Seeing a child drinking from his hands, he threw away his cup from his wallet, saying, "A child has beaten me in plainness of living."

Diogenes was once eating lentils when Aristippus, who lived comfortably by flattering the king, passed by and said, "If you would learn to be subservient to the king, you would not have to live on lentils." Diogenes replied, "If you had learned to live on lentils, you would not have to be subservient to the king."

When asked where he came from, he said, "I am a citizen of the world (cosmopolite)."

Plato had defined Man as a "featherless biped." Diogenes plucked a chicken and brought it into the lecture room, crying, "Behold! I've brought you a man." Plato consequently added "with broad flat nails" to his definition.

Seeing an archer who was a bad shot, he sat down beside the target. When asked why, he said, "In order not to be hit."

When someone asked him what was the proper time for supper, he said, "If you are a rich man, whenever you please; and if you are a poor man, whenever you can."

When asked what was the most beautiful thing in the world, he replied, "Freedom of speech."
