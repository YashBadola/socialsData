When Alexander the Great addressed him with greetings and asked if he wanted anything, Diogenes replied "Yes, stand a little out of my sun."

When someone asked him what was the proper time for lunch, he said, "If a rich man, when you will; if a poor man, when you can."

Diogenes lit a lamp in broad daylight and said, as he went about, "I am looking for a human."

When Plato gave Socrates's definition of man as "featherless biped" and was praised for it, Diogenes plucked a chicken and brought it into Plato's Academy, saying, "Behold! I've brought you a man." After this incident, "with broad flat nails" was added to Plato's definition.

Seeing a child drinking from his hands, Diogenes threw away his cup and remarked, "A child has beaten me in plainness of living."

He once saw the officials of a temple leading away some one who had stolen a bowl belonging to the treasurers, and said, "The great thieves are leading away the little thief."

When asked what he was doing, he replied, "I am rolling my tub so that I may not appear to be the only idler among so many workers."

When asked what was the most beautiful thing in the world, he replied, "Freedom of speech."

When asked what sort of wine he found the best to drink, he replied, "That for which other people pay."

When asked why he went about begging for a statue, he said, "To get practice in being refused."

When asked what he had gained from philosophy, he replied, "This at least, if nothing else -- to be prepared for every fortune."

Someone took him into a magnificent house and warned him not to spit, whereupon having cleared his throat he spat into the man's face, being unable, he said, to find a meaner receptacle.

When asked where he came from, he said, "I am a citizen of the world."

Seeing an archer who was a bad shot, he sat down beside the target. When asked why, he said, "To avoid getting hit."

When asked what is the most wretched thing in life, he said, "An old man destitute."

When asked why people give to beggars but not to philosophers, he said, "Because they think they may one day be lame or blind, but never expect that they will turn to philosophy."

To one who asked what was the right time to marry, Diogenes replied, "For a young man not yet: for an old man never at all."

When asked what he thought of the Megarians, he said, "I would rather be a ram in Megara than a son."

Someone said to him, "Most people laugh at you." "And I," he replied, "laugh at them. The asses kick them, but they do not care; and I do not care when they laugh at me."

He was asked what was the most beautiful thing among men. "Freedom of speech," said he.

Seeing a youth dressing with elaborate care, he said, "If it's for men, you're a fool; if for women, a knave."

When asked what death is, he said, "Nothing; for when we are, death has not come, and when death has come, we are not."

He used to say that he followed the example of the trainers of choruses; for they too set the note a little high, to ensure that the rest should hit the right note.

He once begged alms of a statue, and, when asked why he did so, replied, "To get practice in being refused."
