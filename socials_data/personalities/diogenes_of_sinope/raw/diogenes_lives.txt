Diogenes was kneading dough when Plato came by and said, "If you had paid your court to Dionysius, you would not be kneading dough."
Diogenes replied, "If you had kneaded dough, you would not have paid your court to Dionysius."

Alexander the Great came to visit the philosopher Diogenes of Sinope.
Alexander found him resting in the sunlight.
Alexander, thrilled to meet the famous philosopher, asked if there was any favour he might do for him.
Diogenes replied, "Yes, stand out of my sunlight."

When asked where he came from, he said, "I am a citizen of the world (kosmopolitÄ“s)."

Someone took him into a magnificent house and warned him not to spit, whereupon having cleared his throat he spat into the man's face, being unable, he said, to find a meaner receptacle.

He lit a lamp in broad daylight and said, as he went about, "I am looking for a human."

When Plato gave the definition of man as "featherless biped," and was praised for it, Diogenes plucked a chicken and brought it into Plato's Academy, saying, "Behold! I've brought you a man."
The Academy added "with broad flat nails" to the definition.

Seeing a child drinking from his hands, Diogenes threw away his cup and said, "A child has beaten me in plainness of living."

When asked what was the right time to marry, he replied, "For a young man not yet: for an old man never at all."

He used to beg of statues; and when asked the reason, he replied, "To get practice in being refused."

When asked what he had gained from philosophy, he replied, "This at least, if nothing else -- to be prepared for every fortune."
