When asked where he came from, he said, "I am a citizen of the world (cosmopolite)."

Someone took him into a magnificent house and warned him not to spit, whereupon having cleared his throat he spat into the man's face, being unable, he said, to find a meaner receptacle.

When Alexander the Great stood over him and asked what he could do for him, he answered, "Stand out of my sunlight."

He would go about in full daylight with a lamp; and when asked what he was doing, he would answer, "I am looking for a honest man."

When asked what was the right time to marry, he replied, "For a young man not yet: for an old man never at all."

He used to say that he had seen Plato's lectures being wasted on Dionysius.

Plato had defined Man as an animal, biped and featherless, and was applauded. Diogenes plucked a fowl and brought it into the lecture-room with the words, "Here is Plato's man."

When someone asked him what was the proper time for lunch, he said, "If a rich man, when you will; if a poor man, when you can."

He was observing a child drinking out of his hands, and threw away the cup from his wallet with the words, "A child has beaten me in plainness of living."

He used to say that it was the privilege of the gods to want nothing, and of godlike men to want but little.

When asked what he had gained from philosophy, he replied, "This at least, if nothing else -- to be prepared for every fortune."

Seeing a youth dressing with elaborate care, he said, "If it's for men, you're a fool; if for women, a knave."

When asked what was the most beautiful thing in the world, he replied, "Freedom of speech."

He once begged alms of a statue, and, when asked why he did so, replied, "To get practice in being refused."

When asked why people give to beggars but not to philosophers, he said, "Because they think they may one day become lame or blind, but never expect that they will turn to philosophy."

He was breakfasting in the marketplace, and the bystanders gathered round him with cries of "dog." "It is you who are dogs," cried he, "when you stand round and watch me at my breakfast."

When some one said, "Most people laugh at you," his reply was, "And perhaps the asses laugh at them; but just as they don't care for the asses, so I don't care for them."

He saw a woman bowing before the gods in an unseemly attitude, and wishing to free her of superstition, according to Zoilus of Perga, he went up to her and said, "Are you not afraid, my good woman, that a god may be standing behind you? -- for all things are full of him -- and you may be put to shame?"

He used to call the demagogues the mob's lackeys.

When asked what wine he found pleasant to drink, he replied, "That for which other people pay."

He was asked what was the difference between life and death, and he said, "No difference." "Why then," said the questioner, "do you not die?" "Because," said he, "there is no difference."

He lighted a lamp in broad daylight and said, as he went about, "I am looking for a man."

When a man reproached him for going into unclean places, he said, "The sun too penetrates into privies, but is not polluted by them."

He saw some one performing religious purification, and said, "Unhappy man, don't you know that you can no more get rid of errors of conduct by washing than you can of grammatical errors?"

When asked what was the burden of old age, he replied, "Poverty."

He termed education "an ornament to the rich, and a refuge to the poor."

When asked what was the hardest thing to do, he replied, "To know oneself."

He said that the love of money is the mother-city of all evils.
